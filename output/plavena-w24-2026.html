Context

The set-up. Copper sits at $7,500/t on the LME 3M with concentrate markets tightening faster than refined metal. Treatment and refining charges — the fee smelters earn for converting concentrate into cathode — have collapsed to roughly $22/t, a level that renders marginal Asian smelter capacity loss-making at the tolling margin. Chinese and Indian smelters expanded aggressively into a concentrate pipeline that has not kept pace, and mine-supply disruptions have compounded the squeeze. The result is a structural inversion: refined copper economics look firm while the smelting layer bleeds. This is a margin story, not a price story, and it splits the value chain.

The implication. Smelters dependent on spot concentrate face utilisation cuts; those holding long-term contracts or toll arrangements gain relative advantage. Indian players with flexible feed and toll-smelting optionality can convert this dislocation into captured margin if contract terms reset ahead of the H2 mine-supply window. Chinese smelters carrying the overcapacity burden are the natural shorts. Watch for announced utilisation reductions as the clearing mechanism — once 200–300kt of annualised capacity idles, TC/RC should base. The asymmetry favours integrated and toll-exposed names over pure custom smelters. Full positioning framework in Plavena Trading Prospects p.9.

Deep Dive · The Lead Story

The TC/RC Break: Asia's Smelter Reckoning

Treatment charges have collapsed to $22/t, the clearest signal yet that Asian smelting capacity expanded ahead of the concentrate pipeline. This is a margin crisis layered onto firm refined-copper prices — a split that rewards toll-exposed and integrated names while punishing custom smelters reliant on spot feed. The clearing mechanism is utilisation cuts, and they have not yet arrived at scale.

The arithmetic is unforgiving. A custom smelter buying concentrate at spot and earning $22/t in treatment charges cannot cover conversion costs that run materially higher across most Asian operations. For years, TC/RC at $80–100/t underwrote a build-out of refining capacity, particularly across China and increasingly India, on the assumption that mine supply would scale in tandem. It has not. Disruptions at key copper mines, slower-than-guided ramp profiles, and resilient refined demand have inverted the relationship. The concentrate market is now structurally short relative to the smelting capacity chasing it, and the fee smelters command has been competed away to near-zero economics.

What breaks the cycle is supply rationalisation. Until 200–300kt of annualised smelting capacity idles or cuts utilisation, the bid for concentrate stays irrationally firm and TC/RC stays pinned. Chinese smelters carry the heaviest overcapacity burden and are the natural adjustment mechanism, but state-linked operations are slow to cut. That delay extends the pain but also sharpens the eventual snap-back. For traders, the trade is not directional copper — anchored at $7,500/t — but the spread between the refined and concentrate layers. Long integrated producers and toll-exposed Indian smelters; short pure custom smelters carrying spot feed. The reset, when it comes, will be abrupt.

Where Indian Toll Capacity Captures The Gap

India's copper smelting landscape is smaller than China's but structurally better positioned for this dislocation. Toll-smelting arrangements — where the smelter charges a fixed conversion fee rather than carrying concentrate price risk — insulate operators from the TC/RC collapse if contracts are written on favourable terms. Hindustan Copper and Vedanta's Tuticorin assets sit at the centre of this opportunity. As Chinese custom smelters face margin compression, concentrate sellers seek alternative outlets, and Indian toll capacity with flexible feed can negotiate terms that capture the dislocation. The catalyst to watch is a toll-smelting contract announcement in Q3, which would crystallise the advantage. Procurement heads sourcing cathode should evaluate Indian-origin supply against tightening Chinese availability, as utilisation cuts there will reduce export cathode flows into Asian markets through H2. The window to lock terms is now narrowing.

The risk to the Indian thesis is execution. Expansion clarity at Tuticorin has been delayed before, and toll contracts only deliver if feed security holds. But the directional case is sound: in a concentrate-short world, the party that owns conversion capacity with contracted feed wins. Indian smelters that secure long-term concentrate supply now, ahead of the H2 reset, convert a structural market dislocation into durable margin. This is the asymmetric trade within the copper complex.

Goan Restart Floods A Soft Seaborne Market

India's iron-ore exports jumped 38% month-on-month as Goan mining capacity restarts, adding roughly 4.2Mt to seaborne supply. With 62% Fe anchored at $115/dmt and Chinese steel demand subdued, the timing pressures an already-soft market.

The Goan restart reactivates capacity that sat idle through regulatory and environmental constraints, and its return lands at an awkward moment for seaborne iron ore. Chinese steel mills are running cautious utilisation, and the marginal tonne of Indian ore competes directly with Australian and Brazilian majors who hold cost advantages. NMDC's capacity update and Fortescue's volume guidance both carry read-through here. At $115/dmt, the 62% Fe benchmark sits below where high-cost Indian producers comfortably clear, meaning the export surge is a price-taker dynamic, not a price-setter. Additional supply into a demand-constrained market mechanically pressures the curve, and the seaborne balance loosens through the back half of the year unless Chinese restocking surprises to the upside.

For Indian producers, the export surge is volume-positive but margin-dilutive at current prices. NMDC benefits from scale and grade; smaller Goan operators face thinner economics. The implication for buyers is favourable: ample seaborne availability keeps negotiating leverage with the buy-side through H2. Steel procurement teams should resist forward-locking ore at current levels given the loosening balance and the prospect of further price softness as Indian tonnes ramp.

— India angle

The Goan restart hands Indian and Asian steel buyers negotiating leverage as seaborne ore loosens. Defer forward commitments; the balance favours the buy-side into H2.
