Context

The set-up. Crude and base metals have moved in lockstep for most of this cycle; this week they tore apart. Brent fell 9.3% in five sessions and 28.4% over the month as producer discipline cracked and inventories built. Copper, by contrast, closed flat on the week and remains up 4.6% over four weeks, with aluminium holding a 16.6% YTD gain. The signal is that metals are now trading their own supply-tightness story — smelter overcapacity, concentrate scarcity, collapsing treatment charges — rather than tracking the energy complex. Buyers should treat the energy slide as a procurement window, not a demand all-clear: lock physical base-metal tonnage on dips while crude-linked logistics are cheap. Deep Dive · The Lead Story

Why Copper Stopped Listening To Crude

For two years copper and Brent traded as one macro bet on global growth. This week the correlation snapped: crude lost 28.4% over four weeks while copper gained 4.6%. The divergence is not noise — it reflects a supply-side story unique to metals, where collapsing treatment charges and concentrate scarcity are overriding the demand pessimism dragging energy lower. Asian buyers must reposition.

The mechanics are structural, not sentiment. Global copper smelting capacity — concentrated in China and now expanding across India and Indonesia — has outrun mine supply growth. That imbalance has crushed spot treatment and refining charges (TC/RC) toward $22/t, levels that render marginal smelters loss-making on the conversion step alone. When smelters cannot earn on tolling, they cut utilisation, refined output tightens, and the cathode price firms even as macro growth fears mount. That is precisely the wedge driving copper away from crude: oil is pricing a demand recession, copper is pricing a refining bottleneck. The two cannot stay correlated through a supply shock of this kind.

Crude's own unwind is a discipline story. The four-week 28.4% slide followed visible cracks in producer cohesion and rebuilding inventories — a supply-glut signal, not purely weak demand. That distinction matters: it means the energy drop should not be read as a leading indicator for industrial metals demand collapse. Brent remains up 23.4% YTD even after the rout, underlining how stretched the prior rally had become. For procurement teams, the takeaway is to separate the two complexes in hedging books. Crude exposure should be managed as a freight and input-cost variable; copper exposure should be managed against the refined-supply squeeze, which has a longer and more reliable runway into H2 2026.

Treatment charges are the real copper trade

The TC/RC collapse is the single most actionable signal on the board, and it is invisible to anyone watching only cathode spot. Treatment and refining charges are what smelters earn to convert concentrate into metal; when they fall toward $22/t, the conversion business stops paying. Chinese smelters built capacity aggressively through the last cycle, and that overhang now has no concentrate to feed it. The result is a margin squeeze that forces utilisation cuts, which in turn tightens refined supply and supports the cathode price — the mechanism keeping copper bid while crude bleeds. For Indian smelters, this is an opening: operators with secured concentrate feed and toll contracts capture conversion margin that over-built Chinese capacity is surrendering. Hindustan Copper and Vedanta's Tuticorin assets sit directly in that window, provided feed security holds.

The risk is concentrate availability, not demand. If mine supply disappoints further — through Chilean grade decline or permitting delays — TC/RC stays pinned and the squeeze deepens. Buyers should monitor quarterly smelter benchmark settlements and Chinese utilisation data as the leading tells. A benchmark TC/RC settlement below $30/t for 2026 would confirm the structural break and justify locking refined copper supply now rather than chasing it in H2 at a tighter, costlier market.

Lithium and cobalt: floors holding, catalysts absent

Battery-metal benchmarks sat unchanged again this week — lithium carbonate at $14,000/t, cobalt at $30,000/t — both flagged as cached estimates. The flat tape reflects a market that has stopped falling but found no reason to rise. Supply discipline is forming a floor; demand has yet to confirm it.

The lithium story has shifted from oversupply panic to tentative stabilisation. Producer output cuts and deferred expansions have removed the worst of the surplus pressure, and the carbonate price has stopped making new lows. But a floor is not a recovery. Without a demand impulse — accelerating EV sales in China and India, or a restocking cycle from cathode makers — the benchmark has no upward catalyst and trades sideways. Cobalt mirrors the pattern: supply concentration keeps a structural bid under prices, but soft demand caps upside. For procurement teams, this is an accumulation environment, not a momentum trade. The asymmetry now favours patient buyers building coverage at cycle-low prices rather than waiting for confirmation that arrives only with higher levels.

The implication for buyers is to use the flat tape constructively. Battery-metal prices that neither fall nor rise are rare windows to layer in forward coverage without chasing. Indian cell and cathode manufacturers scaling under domestic incentive schemes face a multi-year demand ramp; securing lithium and cobalt feed at current floors de-risks that build-out. The danger is complacency — when the demand catalyst arrives, these illiquid markets reprice fast, and late buyers pay the premium.

— India angle

India's battery-manufacturing ramp meets a lithium market at a cycle-low floor — a rare alignment. Forward coverage locked now insulates domestic cell makers from the sharp repricing that follows any demand confirmation.
